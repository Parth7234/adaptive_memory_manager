Upgrade from Static Scoring to ARC (Adaptive Replacement Cache)Currently, your Adaptive Manager uses a static equation: Score = α·Recency + β·Frequency + γ·Prediction.The problem with static weights is that workloads change. If a user suddenly opens a heavy gallery app (a "scan" workload), the high volume of new pages will flush out frequently used background apps, destroying your cache hit rate.The Solution (ARC):Instead of static weights, implement an ARC-inspired algorithm. ARC maintains two separate lists:T1 (Recency): Pages accessed only once recently.T2 (Frequency): Pages accessed at least twice.Crucially, ARC maintains a dynamic boundary (p) between them, along with "Ghost Caches" (B1 and B2) that remember the IDs of recently evicted pages. If the system gets a hit in the B1 ghost cache, it mathematically proves the cache was too small for recent items, and automatically expands T1 at the expense of T2. It self-tunes in real-time.2. Implement a ZRAM (Compressed RAM) Paging TierIn mobile OS environments (specifically Android and Linux), swapping directly to flash storage (UFS/NVMe) is the absolute last resort because it is painfully slow and burns battery.The Solution:Introduce ZRAM as a middle-tier in your eviction pipeline. ZRAM is a dedicated block of standard RAM that uses fast compression algorithms (like LZ4 or Zstandard) to shrink data.The Pipeline: Active RAM → ZRAM (Compressed, ~3x space savings) → UFS Storage (Swap).Integration: Instead of evicting an app directly to storage when your ML model predicts a low probability of use, compress its memory pages and move them to ZRAM. Paging out of ZRAM back into active RAM takes microseconds, whereas fetching from UFS takes milliseconds.3. Predictive Page Clustering (Spatial Locality)Right now, your Tier 2 pre-loading "pages the binaries into the OS page cache." But an app binary is huge, and the OS doesn't need all of it to draw the first frame.The Solution:When your LSTM predicts the user will open Maps, don't just fetch the whole APK. Profile the app offline to find which specific 4KB memory pages it always requests during a cold start (this is called Page Clustering).When the ML model fires a >25% confidence trigger, issue madvise() calls only for that specific cluster of startup pages.This drastically reduces the I/O bandwidth required for pre-loading, making it safer to execute even under high memory pressure or low battery.How to apply this to your prototype:To implement this in your memory_manager.py, I would prioritize the ZRAM tier first. It is the easiest to simulate (just add an intermediate cache array with a 0.3x size multiplier and a small decompression latency penalty) and will immediately flatten out your remaining Page Fault latency spikes, getting you well past your 20% Load Time reduction target.
